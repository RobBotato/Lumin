# Typhoon Patterns — Pacific Ocean & South China Sea

## Overview
The Western Pacific basin generates the most intense tropical cyclones globally,
averaging 25 named storms per year. The South China Sea corridor is the most
active shipping lane impacted by typhoons, affecting 40% of global container
traffic annually.

## Verified Impact Data

### Category 2 Typhoons
- Typical shipping delay: 3–5 days per affected route
- Port closure duration: 1–3 days (Manila, Hong Kong, Shenzhen)
- Average financial impact: $800M–1.2B per event
- Historical precedent: Tropical Storm Mara (2024) — 4-day delay, $800M impact

### Category 3 Typhoons
- Typical shipping delay: 5–7 days per affected route
- Port closure duration: 3–5 days
- Average financial impact: $2.1B–3.5B per event
- Historical precedent: Typhoon Goni (2020) — 5-day delay, $1.2B impact
- Historical precedent: Typhoon Mangkhut (2018) — 7-day delay, $2.1B impact

### Category 4+ Typhoons
- Typical shipping delay: 7–14 days per affected route
- Port closure duration: 4–7 days
- Average financial impact: $3.5B–8B per event
- Historical precedent: Typhoon Haiyan (2013) — 8-day delay, $2.8B impact
- Critical: Manila port closed for 5 days, Hong Kong for 4 days

## Region-Specific Patterns
- South China Sea (10°–25°N, 110°–130°E): Peak season July–October
- Luzon Strait (18°–22°N, 120°–122°E): Year-round exposure, highest frequency globally
- 60% of trans-Pacific container traffic passes through typhoon-exposed waters

## Mitigation Effectiveness
- Rerouting via Strait of Malacca (Singapore alternate): Adds 4–5 days transit
- Rerouting via Shanghai (instead of Hong Kong/Shenzhen): Adds 2–3 days transit
- Ships with >48h lead time: 70% successfully reroute with minimal impact
- Ships with <24h warning: 90% experience delays, average $1.5M per vessel
