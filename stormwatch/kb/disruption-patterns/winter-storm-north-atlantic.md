# Winter Storm Patterns — North Atlantic

## Overview
North Atlantic winter storms (November–March) disrupt the busiest shipping lane
globally: the Europe–North America corridor. Rotterdam-bound traffic is most
exposed, with average of 8–12 significant storm events per season.

## Verified Impact Data

### Severe Winter Storms
- Typical shipping delay: 4–8 days for Rotterdam-bound vessels
- Average financial impact: $1.8B–3.2B per significant event
- Historical precedent: Winter Storm Uri (2021) — 5-day delay, $3.2B impact
- Historical precedent: Atlantic Storm Dennis (2020) — 4-day delay, $1.8B impact

## Region-Specific Patterns
- North Atlantic (40°–55°N, 10°–50°W): Peak December–February
- Wave heights regularly exceed 8m, forcing course deviations of 200–400nm
- Rotterdam receives 30% of global container traffic, making it a single point
  of failure during severe North Atlantic weather

## Mitigation Effectiveness
- Southern crossing via Azores (35°–40°N): Adds 2 days transit but avoids
  worst conditions; viable when lead time >48h
- UK port holding: Rotterdam-bound vessels can hold at Southampton/Felixstowe
  for 3–5 days during peak storm conditions
- Advanced routing algorithms reduce weather exposure by 25% when applied 72h+ ahead
